Get Started

FlagBot is the world's first image based flag identifier!

This is a blog (tech) post to explain what FlagBot is, what makes it special, and how it works. If you just want to try it out, it is available now, on Android an iOS. FlagBot is a machine learning algorithm, trained on over 60,000 images.

Who, or what, is FlagBot?

FlagBot is a machine learning algorithm.
Likely you've heard the term AI or artificial intelligence, a concept and topic of science fiction that is extremely popular and timeless. In recent years, computer and data scientists have found we now have enough data and compute power to actually make some of these sci-fi concepts a reality. Some may say machine learning isn't AI, it's just statistics, but then I ask, is your brain not just statistics as well? These are some of the topics that will be addressed in the post.

How does FlagBot learn?

Though there are many types, and techniques for machine learning, FlagBot uses [FINAL TECHNIQUE] to train a neural network to identify which country's flag is in your image. This is done by training FlagBot (a neural network) on thousands (60,000) of images, and doing what's called back propagation. This means that on one end of our neural network, we have our input (an image with a flag) and on the other end and output, the country of the flag. Since we have a label (country) for every training image, we know which output the neural network should give for each image in the training dataset. Everytime the neural network is given an image, depending on how correct the answer is, we forcefully change FlagBot's brain (neural network) so that its answer is more correct.

Is this really intellegence?

Some individuals seem to have reservations about whether neural networks and machine learning are really AI at all. In response to this, I would question whether they actually know what their brain is made of.
Why do we use neural networks in computers, and where did the idea come from? As the name my indicate, a neural network is very similar to what is actually letting you read this right now. Whereas your brain has neurons which fire in accordance to action potentials of ions flowing through your brain, which could be thought of as electricity, FlagBot uses calculations and weights to determine when its neurons fire. Our brains are simply the combination of many, many neural networks feeding into each other, with inputs coming from our senses, and outputs connecting to our muscles. This is how very small animals, with no consciousness are able to move around, eat, and poop. Micheal Stevens shows how you can use humans to transfer the information, rather than neurons, to do simple numeral identfication. These types of small neural networks allow for simple operation. FlagBot is far more complex than a small worm, having thousands of nodes rather than hundreds.

Do animals learn the same way machines do?

Yes! and no...
As we all know by know, thanks to Darwin, and the thousands of specimins catalogued by incredible biologists, animals and life, is the products of evolution. Likely you've heard of the different parts of your brain, and their individual functions, such as the amygdala, which plays a huge role in how you feel every day. These brain structures are evolved neural networks, which have found an optimal solution for operation within humans. Thanks to neuroplacicity, we aren't forced to use the same brain everyday, unlike FlagBot, as our brains change all the time, learning new things, and altering our responses to stimuli. So should machines use evoluion? Yes! and they do! As already mentioned FlagBot learns using backpropagation, not evolution, but that doesn't mean it couldn't. Neuroevolution was one of the first machine learning techniques, as it is simple, and makes sense. Some of my favorites include, MarI/O, Evolution Simulator, and The Coding Train's Flappy Bird. There are now more complex frameworks for evolutionary machine learning such as Uber's EvoGrad. So just because FlagBot uses more traditional machine learning techniques, it doesn't mean that it has to.
